India has 8.8 million people living with dementia and 101 million with Type 2 diabetes — a metabolic-neurological risk population that is largely invisible to primary care. Current gold-standard diagnosis (PET/CSF workup) costs ₹50,000–1,50,000 per patient and is available only in 6–8 metropolitan centres. Meanwhile, India's 1.1 million ASHA workers conduct 450 million home visits annually across 600,000 villages, already collecting the blood pressure, blood glucose, and health history that dementia risk models need.

We propose ScreenBrain: a low-cost AI dementia screening system deployable within an existing ASHA home visit. Inputs — blood biochemistry (HbA1c, BP, BMI, fasting glucose), validated literacy-fair cognitive scores (RUDAS, PHC-CS), and sociodemographic variables — are collectable in under 15 minutes with no imaging required. An XGBoost classifier trained on SANSCOG (10,013 rural Karnataka participants, CDR-staged neurologist ground truth) outputs a Green/Orange/Red risk flag for the ASHA worker and top-3 SHAP-explained risk factors for the PHC physician.

Primary hypothesis (H1): ScreenBrain achieves AUC >0.80 for MCI detection on the SANSCOG test set versus the ACE-III-alone baseline. External validation on LASI-DAD (4,096 participants, 12 Indian language groups) targets AUC >0.70 — demonstrating pan-Indian generalization without retraining.

ScreenBrain is scoped as a population-level risk stratification tool enabling prioritised referral, not a diagnostic replacement. It generates the first AI model for MCI detection trained and validated on an Indian community dementia cohort, using inputs accessible to every ASHA worker in the country.

2Problem Statement
▼

India's Dementia Crisis is a Rural Detection Desert

Of India's 8.8 million people living with dementia, 80% are undiagnosed. The bottleneck is not treatment — it is detection. MRI, PET, and CSF biomarker workups cost ₹50,000–1,50,000 per patient and are unavailable outside 6–8 metropolitan centres. For the 900 million Indians in rural and semi-urban settings, these diagnostics are inaccessible within a lifetime.

The ASHA Infrastructure is Structurally Underutilised

India's 1.1 million ASHA workers conduct 450 million home visits annually across 600,000 villages, already recording blood pressure, blood glucose, BMI, and health history for NHM programs. This is precisely the feature set a low-cost dementia screening model requires. No validated AI tool has been built to use this existing workflow for cognitive risk assessment.

Western Cognitive Tools Fail Indian Populations

The MMSE — the most widely used cognitive screen globally — has a catastrophic failure mode for rural India: 100% of illiterate subjects score below the dementia cutoff, producing false positives for the entire unschooled rural elderly population. RUDAS (Rowland Universal Dementia Assessment Scale) and PHC-CS (Primary Health Care Cognitive Screen) were designed to minimise education and literacy bias, but have never been integrated into an ML pipeline validated against Indian dementia cohort ground truth.

The 14 modifiable risk factors accounting for ~45% of global dementia cases (Lancet Commission 2020) — including diabetes, hypertension, physical inactivity, low education, depression, and social isolation — are all assessable at near-zero marginal cost during an existing ASHA home visit.

India's Metabolic Risk Profile is Uncharacterised

India's T2D onset occurs ~10 years earlier than in Western populations, creating a large cohort of young-onset diabetics accumulating metabolic-neurological risk over decades. The SANSCOG cohort (Ravindranath et al. 2023, 10,013 rural Karnataka participants) is the only published Indian study pairing metabolic blood panels with longitudinal cognitive assessments and CDR-staged neurologist labels — and no ML model has been trained on it for this task.

The Specific Research Gap

No published study has: (1) trained an ML model on SANSCOG or LASI-DAD for MCI detection using ASHA-collectable inputs; (2) validated literacy-fair cognitive tools in an ML pipeline against Indian CDR ground truth; or (3) proposed a deployable AI system integrated with India's existing ASHA home-visit infrastructure. Published non-imaging ML models achieve AUC 0.93–0.94 — but on Western cohorts or single-centre Indian clinic datasets with no external validation on Indian community samples.

3AI Approach & Architecture
▼

Model Choice: XGBoost + SHAP

XGBoost is the evidence-backed choice for tabular clinical data at ~10,000 records: it consistently outperforms deep learning architectures on small tabular datasets, natively handles missing values without imputation hacks, and produces SHAP feature importance scores usable for clinical audit and regulatory review. Deep learning is reserved for Phase 2 speech data requiring sequential modelling.

Missing-Data Robustness

Model trained with random missingness augmentation (30–60% random feature dropout during training). At inference: if HbA1c unavailable, runs on cognitive + demographics; if cognitive test incomplete, runs on blood + demographics. Confidence intervals widen accordingly and are reported alongside the risk flag. The model never refuses to produce output due to missing features.

Output Design

ASHA-facing: Single traffic light (Green / Orange / Red) + one plain-language sentence in Kannada. No numbers. No clinical terminology.
PHC physician-facing: Risk score (0–100) + top-3 SHAP-contributing features with direction (e.g., "High fasting glucose ↑ risk 23%"). SHAP summary plots available for audit.

Phase 2 Roadmap

Upon SANSCOG Wave 2/3 access: Model M (longitudinal conversion prediction) using engineered delta features (ΔACE-III, ΔHbA1c, Δword recall) to predict 2–4 year MCI-to-dementia conversion. Model M activates after 2+ ASHA visits; Model L (ScreenBrain Phase 1) useful from day one.

Train / Test Split

70 / 15 / 15 stratified by CDR stage and SANSCOG wave. LASI-DAD is fully held-out — never used for model selection, tuning, or threshold calibration.

Ground Truth Labels

CDR staging: 0 = cognitively normal, 0.5 = MCI, ≥1 = dementia. Binary classification: MCI + dementia vs. normal (screening use case). Neurologist consensus labels preferred where confirmed in SANSCOG Wave 3.

Pre-Access Synthetic Validation (TSTR)

SYN-1 uses Train-on-Synthetic, Test-on-Real (TSTR): once SANSCOG access is confirmed, retrain on real data and compare AUC. If TSTR ≈ train-on-real, synthetic fidelity is sufficient; if gap >0.05, architecture decisions revisited before publication.

Data Security

All SANSCOG processing on IISc-controlled infrastructure. No external cloud transfer without CBR approval. DPDPA 2023 compliance via anonymisation + local storage. ABHA patient ID integration pathway for longitudinal follow-up tracking.

Acknowledged Limitations

  • CDR staging does not carry the diagnostic weight of biomarker-confirmed (PET/CSF) Alzheimer's pathology — ScreenBrain is explicitly scoped as a risk stratification tool enabling prioritised referral, not a diagnostic replacement.
  • ASHA inter-rater reliability for RUDAS/PHC-CS has no published Cohen's kappa — this study will measure it as a secondary outcome (most important missing evidence in the field).
  • Model performance may degrade for populations with extreme literacy profiles not represented in SANSCOG's rural Karnataka sample.

8India-First Design
▼
  • SANSCOG is irreplaceable: The only rural Karnataka longitudinal cohort pairing metabolic panels with cognitive assessments and CDR neurologist labels. No Western team can replicate this — it requires CBR data access and the Karnataka health ecosystem. This makes ScreenBrain uniquely India-native, not India-adapted.
  • Literacy-fair cognitive tools: RUDAS validated in Kerala (n=116); PHC-CS validated in Karnataka (Kannada). Both designed to minimise education bias — essential for India's rural elderly with 0–5+ years of schooling.
  • ASHA integration: ScreenBrain piggybacks on 450 million existing annual home visits. No new infrastructure. No dedicated screening camps. No additional training beyond a 2-hour RUDAS workshop already piloted in Indian PHC settings.
  • Near-zero marginal cost: Blood tests already performed at PHC. RUDAS/PHC-CS questionnaire adds 8 minutes. Software cost is zero at scale — no consumables, no specialised devices.
  • India's metabolic risk profile: T2D onset 10 years earlier in India creates a young-onset dementia risk cohort that Western models have never been calibrated on. SANSCOG captures this population uniquely.
  • Kannada-native output (Phase 1); multilingual (Phase 2): ASHA-facing output in Kannada in Phase 1. Phase 2 expands via IndicWav2Vec to 40+ Indian languages, aligning with MeitY Bhashini platform.
  • Regulatory alignment: DPDPA 2023 compliance via anonymisation + local storage. No cloud transfer of SANSCOG data without CBR approval. ABHA patient ID integration pathway for longitudinal tracking across ASHA visits.
