India has 8.8 million people living with dementia and 101 million with Type 2 diabetes — a metabolic-neurological risk population largely invisible to primary care. Current gold-standard diagnosis (PET/CSF workup) costs ₹50,000–1,50,000 per patient and is available only in 6–8 metropolitan centres.

We propose ScreenBrain: a low-cost AI screening system deployable within an existing ASHA home visit. Inputs — blood biochemistry, validated literacy-fair cognitive scores, and sociodemographic variables — are collectable in under 15 minutes with no imaging required. An XGBoost classifier trained on SANSCOG (10,013 rural Karnataka participants, CDR-staged neurologist ground truth) outputs a Green/Orange/Red risk flag for the ASHA worker and top-3 SHAP-explained risk factors for the PHC physician.

Primary Hypothesis H1: AUC >0.80 for MCI detection on SANSCOG test set vs. ACE-III-alone baseline. H2: AUC >0.70 on LASI-DAD external test (4,096 participants, 12 Indian language groups) without retraining.

ScreenBrain is scoped as a population-level risk stratification tool enabling prioritised referral, not a diagnostic replacement. It generates the first AI model for MCI detection trained and validated on an Indian community dementia cohort.

02 Problem Statement ▾

India's Dementia Crisis is a Rural Detection Desert

Of India's 8.8 million people living with dementia, 80% are undiagnosed. The bottleneck is detection, not treatment. MRI, PET, and CSF biomarker workups cost ₹50,000–1,50,000 and are unavailable outside 6–8 metropolitan centres. For the 900 million Indians in rural and semi-urban settings, these diagnostics are inaccessible within a lifetime.

The ASHA Infrastructure is Structurally Underutilised

India's 1.1 million ASHA workers conduct 450 million home visits annually across 600,000 villages, already recording blood pressure, blood glucose, BMI, and health history for NHM programmes. This is precisely the feature set a low-cost dementia screening model requires. No validated AI tool has been built to use this existing workflow for cognitive risk assessment.

Western Cognitive Tools Fail Indian Populations

The MMSE — most widely deployed cognitive screen globally — has a catastrophic failure mode: 100% of illiterate subjects score below the dementia cutoff, producing false positives for the entire unschooled rural elderly population. RUDAS and PHC-CS correct for literacy bias but have never been integrated into an ML pipeline validated against Indian dementia cohort ground truth.

Research Gap The 14 modifiable risk factors accounting for ~45% of global dementia cases (Lancet Commission 2020) — diabetes, hypertension, physical inactivity, low education, depression — are all assessable at near-zero marginal cost during an existing ASHA home visit. No published study has built an AI system that uses them.

India's Metabolic Risk Profile is Uncharacterised

India's T2D onset occurs ~10 years earlier than in Western populations. SANSCOG (Ravindranath et al. 2023, 10,013 rural Karnataka participants) is the only published Indian study pairing metabolic blood panels with longitudinal cognitive assessments and CDR-staged neurologist labels — and no ML model has been trained on it for MCI detection.

03 AI Approach & Architecture ▾

Model Choice

XGBoost consistently outperforms deep learning on tabular clinical data at ~10,000 records, natively handles missing values, and produces SHAP feature importance scores for clinical audit. Deep learning is reserved for Phase 2 speech data requiring sequential modelling.

Missing-Data Robustness

Model trained with 30–60% random missingness augmentation. If HbA1c unavailable, runs on cognitive and demographics only. If cognitive test incomplete, runs on blood and demographics. Confidence intervals widen accordingly and are reported alongside the risk flag.

Phase 2 Roadmap Upon SANSCOG Wave 2/3 access: Model M (longitudinal conversion prediction) using engineered delta features (ΔACE-III, ΔHbA1c, Δword recall) to predict 2–4 year MCI-to-dementia conversion. Activates after 2+ ASHA visits.

Acknowledged Limitations

  • CDR staging does not carry the diagnostic weight of biomarker-confirmed (PET/CSF) Alzheimer's pathology. ScreenBrain is explicitly scoped as risk stratification, not diagnosis.
  • ASHA inter-rater reliability for RUDAS/PHC-CS has no published Cohen's kappa. This study will measure it as a secondary outcome.
  • Performance may degrade for populations with extreme literacy profiles not represented in SANSCOG's rural Karnataka sample.

08 India-First Design ▾
  • SANSCOG is irreplaceable. The only rural Karnataka longitudinal cohort pairing metabolic panels with cognitive assessments and CDR labels. Requires CBR access — no Western team can replicate this.
  • Literacy-fair cognitive tools. RUDAS validated in Kerala (n=116); PHC-CS validated in Karnataka (Kannada). Designed for 0–5+ years of schooling in India's rural elderly population.
  • ASHA integration. Piggybacks on 450 million existing annual home visits. No new infrastructure. No dedicated screening camps.
  • Near-zero marginal cost. Blood tests already at PHC. RUDAS/PHC-CS adds 8 minutes. Software cost is zero at scale — no consumables, no specialised devices.
  • India's metabolic risk profile. T2D onset 10 years earlier creates a young-onset dementia risk cohort unrepresented in Western training data.
  • Kannada-native output (Phase 1); 40+ languages (Phase 2). IndicWav2Vec multilingual; MeitY Bhashini platform alignment.
  • Regulatory alignment. DPDPA 2023 compliance; no cloud transfer without CBR approval; ABHA patient ID integration for longitudinal tracking.
