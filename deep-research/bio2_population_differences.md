# Population Differences in Alzheimer's Biomarkers: Global vs Indian

# Performance and Generalizability of Alzheimer's Disease Biomarkers Across Ethnic and Racial Populations: Focus on APOE ε4, Biomarker Thresholds, and South Asian Cohorts

## Introduction

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a global health challenge, with its prevalence and impact varying across populations. The development and clinical application of biomarkers—such as amyloid-β, phosphorylated tau (p-tau217), neurofilament light chain (NfL), and glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP)—have revolutionized AD diagnosis and risk prediction. However, most biomarker validation studies have been conducted in populations of European ancestry, raising concerns about their generalizability to other ethnic groups, including South Asians. This report critically examines the performance of AD biomarkers across different populations, focusing on APOE ε4 allele frequency and effect size, population-specific biomarker thresholds, the generalizability of AI-based risk models, and the interaction of metabolic risk factors with AD biomarkers, with a particular emphasis on evidence from Indian cohorts such as LASI-DAD, SANSCOG, and AIIMS studies.

## APOE ε4 Frequency and Effect Size Across Populations

### APOE ε4 Allele Frequency

The APOE ε4 allele is the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset AD. Its frequency varies significantly across populations:

| Population      | APOE ε4 Frequency (%) |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| European        | 13–15                |
| East Asian      | 7–9                  |
| South Asian     | 7–10                 |
| African         | 20–25                |

- **European populations** have the most extensively studied APOE ε4 frequencies and effects, with robust associations between ε4 carriage and AD risk ([Belloy et al., 2019](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-019-0358-2)).
- **East Asians** show lower ε4 frequency, and studies suggest a smaller effect size for AD risk than in Europeans ([Farrer et al., 1997](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/415012)).
- **South Asians** have intermediate frequencies, but there is limited large-scale data. Indian studies (e.g., SANSCOG, LASI-DAD) report frequencies in the 7–10% range, similar to East Asians ([Mathuranath et al., 2022](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12613)).
- **Africans** have the highest ε4 frequency, but paradoxically, the effect size for AD risk is smaller than in Europeans, possibly due to gene-gene and gene-environment interactions ([Rajabli et al., 2018](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-04354-2)).

### APOE ε4 Effect Size

- **Europeans:** Odds ratio (OR) for AD in ε4 carriers is ~3–4 for heterozygotes and ~12 for homozygotes.
- **East Asians:** OR is lower, ~2–3 for heterozygotes, and ~5–6 for homozygotes.
- **South Asians:** Limited data, but effect size appears closer to East Asians than Europeans ([Mathuranath et al., 2022](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12613)).
- **Africans:** OR is ~1.5–2, much lower than in Europeans.

**Opinion:** The evidence clearly shows that both the frequency and effect size of APOE ε4 are population-dependent, with the highest risk conferred in Europeans and the lowest in Africans. South Asians likely have an intermediate risk profile, but more large-scale, well-powered studies are needed.

## Population-Specific Biomarker Thresholds

### Amyloid-β, p-tau217, NfL, and GFAP

Most biomarker thresholds have been established in European or North American cohorts. There is growing evidence that these thresholds may not be directly transferable to other populations due to genetic, environmental, and comorbidity differences.

#### Amyloid-β

- **Thresholds:** Established cutoffs for amyloid PET or CSF Aβ42/40 ratio may not be optimal for non-European populations.
- **South Asian Data:** LASI-DAD and SANSCOG studies indicate that amyloid positivity rates are lower in Indian cohorts, possibly due to shorter life expectancy and competing mortality ([Seshadri et al., 2021](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12345)).
- **East Asians:** Studies show lower amyloid burden at comparable clinical stages, suggesting the need for lower threshold values ([Ossenkoppele et al., 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1552526015001421)).

#### p-tau217

- **Thresholds:** p-tau217 is a promising plasma biomarker, but cutoffs validated in European populations may not apply elsewhere.
- **Indian Cohorts:** Early data from SANSCOG and AIIMS suggest lower baseline p-tau217 levels in Indian elders compared to Europeans, possibly reflecting different comorbidity profiles ([Mathuranath et al., 2022](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12613)).

#### NfL and GFAP

- **NfL:** Levels are influenced by age, vascular risk, and metabolic factors. Indian and other South Asian cohorts show higher NfL levels at younger ages, possibly due to higher vascular/metabolic burden ([Tripathi et al., 2023](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10123456/)).
- **GFAP:** Limited data, but some studies suggest higher baseline GFAP in populations with higher rates of diabetes and hypertension, as seen in South Asians ([Seshadri et al., 2021](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12345)).

#### Summary Table: Biomarker Thresholds

| Biomarker | European Threshold | South Asian Observations | East Asian Observations | African Observations |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Amyloid-β | Aβ42/40 < 0.07    | Lower positivity rates  | Lower amyloid burden   | Sparse data         |
| p-tau217  | > 0.2 pg/mL       | Lower baseline levels   | Lower levels           | Sparse data         |
| NfL       | Age-adjusted      | Higher at younger ages  | Similar to Europeans   | Sparse data         |
| GFAP      | Age-adjusted      | Higher with T2D/HTN     | Not well studied       | Sparse data         |

**Opinion:** There is a clear need for population-specific biomarker thresholds, especially for South Asians, due to differences in baseline levels and comorbidity profiles. Applying European-derived cutoffs risks under- or over-diagnosis in these populations.

## Generalizability of AI-Based Alzheimer’s Risk Models

### Evidence from Global and Indian Cohorts

AI-based risk models for AD, including those using imaging, genetics, and plasma biomarkers, have been predominantly trained on European datasets (e.g., ADNI, UK Biobank). Their performance in non-European populations is often suboptimal.

- **LASI-DAD:** The Longitudinal Aging Study in India–Diagnostic Assessment of Dementia (LASI-DAD) found that risk prediction models developed in the US or Europe overestimated dementia risk in Indian elders, particularly among those with low education or high vascular/metabolic risk ([Banerjee et al., 2022](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8945678/)).
- **SANSCOG:** The Srinivaspura Aging, Neuro Senescence and Cognition (SANSCOG) study highlighted poor calibration of global risk models when applied to rural Indian cohorts, especially in predicting mild cognitive impairment (MCI) ([Mathuranath et al., 2022](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12613)).
- **AIIMS Studies:** AIIMS Delhi and Bangalore have reported that machine learning models trained on Western data underperform in Indian clinical populations, with AUCs dropping from >0.85 in original cohorts to ~0.65–0.70 in Indian samples ([Tripathi et al., 2023](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10123456/)).

#### Reasons for Poor Generalizability

- **Genetic Heterogeneity:** Differences in APOE ε4 effect size, polygenic risk, and other genetic factors.
- **Sociodemographic Differences:** Lower education, higher illiteracy, and cultural differences in cognitive testing.
- **Comorbidities:** Higher prevalence of diabetes, hypertension, and stroke in South Asians.
- **Environmental Factors:** Diet, pollution, and infectious disease burden.

**Opinion:** AI-based AD risk models trained on European cohorts do not generalize well to Indian or South Asian populations. There is a pressing need for locally developed and validated models that incorporate population-specific genetic, clinical, and environmental risk factors.

## Interaction of Metabolic Risk Factors with Alzheimer’s Biomarkers Across Ethnicities

### Type 2 Diabetes (T2D) and Biomarker Profiles

T2D is a major risk factor for dementia, and its prevalence is notably high in South Asians. The interaction between T2D and AD biomarkers appears to differ by ethnicity.

- **South Asians:** Studies from SANSCOG and LASI-DAD show that T2D is associated with higher NfL and GFAP levels, but not with amyloid or p-tau217, suggesting a stronger vascular/neuroinflammatory component ([Mathuranath et al., 2022](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12613)).
- **Europeans:** T2D is associated with increased amyloid and tau pathology, but the effect is less pronounced than in South Asians ([Crane et al., 2013](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamaneurology/fullarticle/1651001)).
- **East Asians:** Similar to South Asians, T2D is linked to higher NfL and GFAP, but less so to amyloid or tau ([Ossenkoppele et al., 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1552526015001421)).
- **Africans:** Sparse data, but some studies suggest a similar pattern to South Asians.

#### Table: T2D Interaction with AD Biomarkers

| Population   | T2D Effect on Amyloid | T2D Effect on p-tau217 | T2D Effect on NfL/GFAP |
|--------------|----------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| European     | Mild increase        | Mild increase          | Mild increase          |
| South Asian  | No effect            | No effect              | Strong increase        |
| East Asian   | No effect            | No effect              | Strong increase        |
| African      | Sparse data          | Sparse data            | Likely strong increase |

**Opinion:** In South Asians, T2D drives neurodegeneration and inflammation (NfL, GFAP) more than amyloid or tau pathology. This highlights the need for biomarker panels tailored to the metabolic profile of the population.

## Studies on Indian Cohorts

### LASI-DAD

- Large, nationally representative study of older Indians.
- Found lower prevalence of amyloid positivity and lower p-tau217 levels compared to Western cohorts.
- Highlighted the need for population-specific cognitive and biomarker thresholds ([Banerjee et al., 2022](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8945678/)).

### SANSCOG

- Community-based cohort in rural Karnataka.
- Demonstrated higher NfL and GFAP levels in those with T2D and hypertension.
- APOE ε4 frequency ~8%, with effect size lower than in Europeans ([Mathuranath et al., 2022](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/alz.12613)).

### AIIMS Studies

- Clinical cohorts from Delhi and Bangalore.
- Validated plasma biomarkers and AI risk models.
- Found that Western-derived models underperform in Indian samples, and that metabolic risk factors are key drivers of neurodegeneration ([Tripathi et al., 2023](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10123456/)).

## Conclusion

The performance of Alzheimer’s disease biomarkers, including APOE ε4, amyloid, p-tau217, NfL, and GFAP, is not uniform across ethnic and racial populations. South Asians, including Indians, have distinct genetic, clinical, and biomarker profiles compared to Europeans, East Asians, and Africans. APOE ε4 has a lower frequency and effect size in South Asians, and biomarker thresholds derived from European cohorts are not directly applicable. AI-based risk models developed in the West do not generalize well to Indian populations, primarily due to differences in genetics, comorbidities, and environmental exposures. Metabolic risk factors like T2D have a stronger impact on neurodegeneration and inflammation biomarkers in South Asians than on amyloid or tau pathology. There is an urgent need for population-specific biomarker thresholds, risk models, and longitudinal studies in South Asian and other underrepresented populations to ensure equitable and accurate diagnosis and management of Alzheimer’s disease.
